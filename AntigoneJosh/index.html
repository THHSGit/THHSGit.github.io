July 12, 1997, was a day of mixed feelings for the Yousafzai family of Mingora, Swat Valley, Pakistan. Ziauddin and Tor Pekai Yousafzai had just given birth to a girl at dawn. Normally for Pashtuns, the day a girl is born is a very sad occasion, as girls were not considered useful for carrying on a family name. However, she was born as the last star of the night faded away, which is considered to be an auspicious sign in her religion. Her father knew that she would be different than other children and began to educate her from her birth. From a very young age, Malala had a thirst for education, unmatched by many others. She looked forward to the day she would be able to enter a school and fill herself with even more knowledge than she already attained. Her father himself owned an educational institution and was a substantial advocate for education for girls. However, her future, almost guaranteed to be successful, all changed when the Taliban took over Swat Valley in 2007 and began imposing strict Muslim law. This wouldn't seem substantial, but it involved the banning of education for all girls. By 2009, more than 400 girls schools were shut down, some blown up in the process. Television, music and other cultural activities that were banned as well. Through all of these actions, the Taliban had succeeded in spreading terror to many families in Swat valley. Malala’s family was certainly not included on this list. Later in 2009, at the age of 11, Malala appeared on a Pakistani talk show with her talk titled “How dare the Taliban take away my basic right to education?". In addition to this, she began blogging under a pseudonym for BBC’s Urdu page, describing the conditions in Swat Valley and her thoughts about the Taliban’s banning of girls education. During this time period, her family was forced to flee Swat Valley for a period of time due to the oncoming threat of war. When she returned, she continued to blog and appear on talk shows about the issue. At the end of 2009, between her blogging and appearances on talk shows, it became apparent who she really was. Two events occurred as a result of her revealed identity. She won the Pakistani National Youth Peace Prize, a great honor, but she was also sent a death threat from the Taliban. She continued to protest and attend her father’s school; but on October 9, 2012, her life changed when Taliban gunmen shot her in a school bus as she returned from school.

Since the age of 11, Malala Yousafzai has been involved in activist work for female education rights. Malala created a public blog, on which she wrote about her life under Taliban and her thoughts on women’s education rights in her country. She was profiled in a NY times documentary and then became famous through her craft of giving speeches and promoting education for women. Tragically, Malala was shot by Taliban and was close to death. However, she survived and continued to do activist work and fight for what she believed in. In 2015, Malala opened a school for women from 14-18. The malala petition, created and started by the UN Special Envoy for Global Education. Many people signed this petition, in which it caused the UN to enable a course in primary schooling for children. This petition also led to the creation of the Right to Education Bill in Pakistan which allowed children to have access to free education. In addition, Malala held the first ever youth UN takeover and brought together many young leaders. The youth now has a say and a voice in politics. In addition, Malala has written 4 books about her life. In 2013, she wrote the book I am Malala: The Girl who Stood up For Education. This book has been sold about 1.8 million copies and counting. It has left a huge impact on the world and caused many people to change their stance on education for women. The second book Malala wrote was called I am Malala: How one Girl Stood up for Education and changed the world. The third book that she wrote was in 2017 Malala’s Magic Pencil. These books have all sold millions of copies and impacted the world in more ways than one. These books have inspired young people to have a voice in their society and voice their opinions and stand up for what they think is right.

Malala is very young being just 20, but she has accomplished more already than many people can in a lifetime. As a Pakistani activist, she has made a huge impact on the world by breaking a law that she knew was sexist and extremely unfair. The Taliban had taken over Pakistan and implemented new rules such as girls were not allowed to go to school. Malala, born to a family that valued learning, wanted for girls to be able to go to school and thus, she defied Taliban. Unfortunately, girls who tried to rebel were attacked on their way to school as a way to scare them off. Although Malala heard about the dangers of getting an education, she went to school, attracting the government’s attention and therefore, receiving a death threat. Fearlessly, she continued to go to school and sadly on October 9, 2012, she was shot in the head by a gunman. She managed to barely escape her death and fought through all the obstacles in her way. She continued to advocate for women’s education in speeches, movements, and through her book “I am Malala” while hearing criticism from others. Being only 17, she received the Nobel Peace Prize, making her the youngest person to ever get the award. Other awards she has won include: National Malala Peace Prize, Sakharov Prize, Goodreads Choice Awards Best Memoir & Autobiography I Am Malala: The Girl Who Stood Up for Education and Was Shot by the Taliban, Simone de Beauvoir Prize, United Nations Prize in the Field of Human Rights, Ambassador of Conscience Award, Mother Teresa Awards, Glamour Award for The Girls' Hero, Shorty Award for Teen Hero. Moreover, her perseverance and standing up for her beliefs resulted in so much support from some of the people of the world and her actions and movement towards female education are really appreciated as they have made a huge impact already.

After Malala Yousafzai was shot, she was rushed to the military hospital in Peshawar. She was in critical condition and part of her skull had to be removed due to her swelling brain. Malala was then transferred to a hospital in Birmingham, England. She had multiple surgeries on her facial nerves in order to fix the left side of her face that was paralyzed due to the accident. When Malala recovered, she protested more strongly than ever and decided to “get back on her horse” to accomplish her goal of getting educational rights for girls all around the world. She created a foundation called Malala Funds, which uses the donations received to help girls without secondary education in places such as Syria and Jordan. In order to bring her opinion to the public, she gave an inspirational speech at the United Nations on her 19th birthday in 2013 and this day became known as “Malala Day.” She also made a speech at Harvard University in 2013 that inspired others to join her in the cause. In addition to this, Malala had an live chat with Sheryl Sandberg on Facebook in August 2014, where she shared her story to the world. This help spread her experiences to others and influence other girls up stand up for their rights. On her 18th birthday in 2015, Malala used the funds from Malala Foundation to open a school for Syrian refugees that provided education for girls ages 14 to 18. It brought a huge turning point in their lives and led them on the right track to receive the rightful education girls should be having. She traveled to places such as London to share out her thoughts and gain support for her Now, she is 20 years old and attends Oxford University for economic, politics, and philosophy. Although she is busy with her college work, Malala still spends some of her time to fight for her original belief.

Connection to Antigone: In both of these cases, Malala and Antigone were both forced to listen and abide to laws they did not like, and they stood up for their rights and other people’s rights. Antigone believed that her brother had been disgraced and that she should have the right to bury him. Malala believed that all girls should have the right to a full education and the decision of having an education should not be determined by the gender of the person. Both of these women were strong and independent and fought for a cause they believed in. Even though these women knew the fatal consequences, they prevailed to work for their beliefs. Even when close to death, these experiences brought out the best in Malala and Antigone.

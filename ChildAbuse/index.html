Our Reaserch

Most child sex offenders have been sexually abused as children themselves: “... sexual abuse during childhood is a specific developmental risk factor that can result in the emergence of a sexual arousal to children.” (Sullivan and Sheehan 76). In a study conducted by Sullivan and Sheehan, key life experiences that the offenders described that they believed contributed to their behavior were: exposure to neglect, physical violence, and experiences of emotional abuse. One of the causes of child abuse is emotional loneliness due to being bullied as a child or bullying as a child. Bullying as a child leads to power problems, as well. As a result of facing abuse as a child, adults develop distorted perceptions of life or life theories such as ““Ward and Keenan (1999)...‘children can give adults more acceptance and love than other adults’; ‘You can’t trust adults’” (Burn and Brown 230).One of the other links to abusing children in the future to the perpetrator’s childhood is criminal activity as a child. Criminal behavior when young causes antisocial behaviors (sexual abuse of a child) (Ward 2003). There are four attachment styles. Attachment styles are the way and type of relationships people form with other people. The four attachment styles are: Secure attachment: (“positive adaptive view of self and others”). preoccupied attachment (“negative view of self, positive view on others”), dismissing attachment (“positive view on self, negative view on others”), and fearful attachment: (“negative view on self and others”). When children face problems with adults or, “when attachment figures are not supportive or available, children develop negative internal working models that contribute to insecurity in attachment relationships” (Wood and Riggs et al. 377). Rapists are most likely to have dismissing attachment, whereas child molesters are most likely to have fearful or preoccupied attachment. Specifically, ”..childhood history of poor family relationships...(Boswell, 1995; Falshaw & Browne, 1997). According to Beech and Mitchell (2005), these adverse childhood experiences make it likely that most sex offenders have developed maladaptive attachment styles, which prompt them to seek intimacy in inappropriate ways, such as confusing sex with intimacy or having sex with children.” (Wood and Riggs 378). Therefore, supportive and non-abusive parent figures are crucial in creating secure adult attachment styles and reducing sexual attraction to children. Moreover, theory of mind (“...reflective function children develop that enable them to make attributions about the mental states of self and others (Fonagy, Gergely, Jurist, & Target, 2002; Fonagy, Redfern, & Charman, 1997)”) capacity is larger in secure individuals. The lack of theory of mind capacity in insecure individuals and “insecure attachment relationships” is a factor in the “maladaptive implicit theories” that sex offenders develop about themselves, others, and the world, during childhood. These theories create cognitive distortions about sex. An implicit theory is defined as the the biases a person uses to form impressions about other people based on limited information. The other people would be the children that the abusers abuse. Implicit theories justify the actions of the abusers in their own minds. Cognitive distortions are the ways that people’s brains convince them that something that is not true is true. They are used to rationalize, minimize or deny behaviors of abusing a child (Abel et al., 1989; Lanning, 2010; Murphy, 1990; Ward, 2000). The cycle of child abuse also relates to social and cultural factors: “Paradoxically, Punjabi parents also believe that in later childhood their children may well need to be physically punished by their teachers if they fail to learn in school.” This belief that punishment is not only okay but necessary and a positive thing roots from cultural norms from the past that have continued onto the present. To add on, the text states,“By contrast, Punjabi parents come from a traditional and well established culture in rural India which emphasizes ‘respect’ towards parents (and to adults generally).” (Frude 72). Abuse is common in some cultures from generation to generation, and it persists. When the abused child grows up, they find it perfectly fine to abuse their own children because that is the cultural norm. The main target or the demand for our work are children that have suffered from maltreatment, in hopes to stop the cycle of abuse. We hope to raise a generous amount of money and donate the money to organizations or projects for abused children that can help them cope correctly with the psychological issues they have faced.
